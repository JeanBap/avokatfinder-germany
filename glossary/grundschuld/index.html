Grundschuld (Land Charge)

A Grundschuld is Germany's main form of real estate security interest — a land charge registered in the Grundbuch used to secure mortgage lending, distinct from a Hypothek (mortgage).

TL;DR A Grundschuld is Germany's main form of real estate security interest — a land charge registered in the Grundbuch used to secure mortgage lending, distinct from a Hypothek (mortgage).

The Grundschuld (land charge) is the primary instrument used to secure bank lending against real estate in Germany. Unlike a traditional mortgage (Hypothek), which is directly tied to the underlying debt and extinguishes automatically when the debt is repaid, a Grundschuld exists independently of any specific loan. It can be reused for future borrowing once created, making it flexible for lenders and borrowers alike.

When purchasing with a mortgage in Germany, the bank requires a Grundschuld to be registered in the Grundbuch as security. The Grundschuld notarial deed is prepared by the notary simultaneously with the Kaufvertrag or shortly after. The notary submits both for registration. The bank typically requires the Grundschuld to be registered before releasing mortgage funds.

The Grundschuld amount is usually set at 100–120% of the loan to account for interest, costs, and potential enforcement costs. It does not automatically reduce as the loan is repaid — it remains at the original amount until formally discharged (Löschung) by a notarial act after the loan is fully repaid. This is an important distinction from UK-style mortgages.

A key feature of the Grundschuld is the Unterwerfungsklausel (submission to immediate enforcement clause) — by signing the Grundschuld deed, the borrower consents to enforcement of the property by the bank without a court judgment if they default. This gives German lenders a powerful and swift enforcement mechanism compared to many other European countries.

When buying a property subject to a seller's existing Grundschuld, the seller must arrange for the Grundschuld to be discharged (gelöscht) from the Grundbuch before or at completion. Buyers should verify the discharge has been effected and refuse to release purchase funds until the Grundbuch confirms a clean title. The notary typically coordinates this process.

Key Facts

NatureAbstract land charge — independent of underlying debt
PurposeSecures mortgage and other bank lending
AmountSet at 100–120% of loan — does not reduce with repayments
EnforcementUnterwerfungsklausel allows immediate enforcement without court order
DischargeSeparate Löschung required after loan repaid
⚠ Common Mistake: Assuming the seller's Grundschuld is automatically discharged on sale. It must be actively cancelled (gelöscht) by the seller's bank via a notarial act. A property can remain encumbered for months after sale if the seller's lender is slow. Verify the Grundbuch is clear before releasing funds.
💡 Expert Tip: Ask your notary to include a payment instruction clause in the Kaufvertrag specifying that part of the purchase price will be paid directly to the seller's bank to discharge the existing Grundschuld — eliminating the risk that the seller pockets the proceeds without clearing the debt.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a Grundschuld and a Hypothek?

A Hypothek (mortgage) is accessory to the loan — it exists only as long as the debt exists and reduces as repayments are made. A Grundschuld is abstract — it exists independently of any specific debt and remains at full value regardless of repayments. Grundschuld is used for nearly all German mortgage lending because its flexibility is commercially preferred by banks.

Can I transfer my Grundschuld to a new property?

No. A Grundschuld is specific to the land parcel it is registered against. It cannot be transferred to a different property. If you sell the property and buy another, a new Grundschuld must be registered on the new property, incurring new notary and land registry fees.

What happens to the Grundschuld when I sell?

The seller must either arrange for the Grundschuld to be deleted (gelöscht) before completion, or the buyer can assume the Grundschuld (Grundschuldübernahme) if the bank consents and the buyer is using the same lender. The former is more common. The notary coordinates the discharge and confirms the Grundbuch is clear before releasing purchase funds.
