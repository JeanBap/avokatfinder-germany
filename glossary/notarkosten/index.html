Notarkosten (Notary and Registration Fees)

German notary and land registry fees — regulated charges payable on every real estate transaction, calculated as a percentage of the purchase price under the GNotKG fee schedule.

Home › Legal Glossary › Notarkosten (Notary and Registration Fees)
TL;DR German notary and land registry fees — regulated charges payable on every real estate transaction, calculated as a percentage of the purchase price under the GNotKG fee schedule.

Notarkosten (notary fees) and Grundbuchkosten (land registry fees) are mandatory costs in every German real estate transaction. Both are regulated by the Gerichts- und Notarkostengesetz (GNotKG — Court and Notary Fees Act) and are calculated as a sliding percentage of the transaction value. Together, they typically amount to 1.5–2% of the purchase price.

The notary fees cover: the notarial certification of the Kaufvertrag (Beurkundungsgebühr), coordination of the land registry entry, management of the escrow process (Notaranderkonto), and all correspondence with the Finanzamt and Grundbuchamt. The fee is non-negotiable — all German notaries charge the same regulated rate for the same services.

Land registry (Grundbuch) fees are charged separately by the Grundbuchamt (land registry) for registering the Auflassungsvormerkung (priority notice) and the final Auflassung (title transfer). Each registration incurs a separate fee. The combined Grundbuch fees typically amount to 0.5–1% of the purchase price.

On a €400,000 property, expect to pay approximately €3,000–4,000 in notary fees and €2,000–3,000 in land registry fees — totalling approximately €5,000–7,000. These are in addition to the Grunderwerbsteuer (transfer tax), estate agent commission, and any legal advice fees. German acquisition costs (Kaufnebenkosten) typically total 10–15% of the purchase price.

If a mortgage (Grundschuld) is also being registered, additional notary and land registry fees apply for the mortgage deed and its registration — typically 0.5–1% of the mortgage amount. First-time buyers and expats often underestimate total acquisition costs. Always request a full Notarkostenvoranschlag (fee estimate) from the notary before signing.

⚠ Common Mistake: Budgeting only 10% for German acquisition costs. The typical total (transfer tax 3.5–6.5% + notary 1.5–2% + agent 3.57% + registration) is 8–15% depending on the state. Always build in the full amount before committing.
💡 Expert Tip: Request a Notarkostenvoranschlag (fee estimate) from the notary before the appointment. Reputable notaries provide this as a matter of course. If a notary refuses to estimate fees in advance, consider using another.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are notary fees tax deductible in Germany?

Notary fees paid on a property used to generate rental income are generally deductible as Anschaffungsnebenkosten (acquisition ancillary costs) in the German income tax return, spread over the depreciation period of the property. Fees on a primary residence are not directly deductible. Consult a German Steuerberater (tax adviser) for your specific situation.

Who pays the notary fees — buyer or seller?

In Germany, the buyer pays all notary and land registry fees by convention, though this can be negotiated in individual cases. The Grunderwerbsteuer is also paid by the buyer. The seller typically bears the cost of discharging any existing Grundschuld (land charge) on the property.

Can I use a notary in a different city from the property?

Yes. German notaries have national jurisdiction — you can use any licensed notary anywhere in Germany for a property transaction anywhere in Germany. However, it is common to use a notary local to the property, as they may have local knowledge and can manage the process more efficiently.
