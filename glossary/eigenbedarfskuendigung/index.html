Eigenbedarfskündigung (Personal Use Eviction)

The German landlord's right to terminate a residential tenancy by claiming the property is needed for their own personal use or for close family members.

Home › Legal Glossary › Eigenbedarfskündigung (Personal Use Eviction)
TL;DR The German landlord's right to terminate a residential tenancy by claiming the property is needed for their own personal use or for close family members.

The Eigenbedarfskündigung (personal use eviction) is the mechanism under German tenancy law allowing a landlord to terminate a rental agreement by claiming they need the property for their own use or for a close family member or household member. It is one of the few grounds on which a German landlord can terminate an indefinite tenancy, and it is subject to strict requirements and proportionality controls.

The landlord must specify the exact person for whom the property is needed and their genuine need for it. The courts have defined the circle of qualifying persons narrowly — typically limited to the landlord themselves, their spouse or partner, their children, parents, siblings, or household staff. Extended relatives (cousins, in-laws) require stronger justification.

The notice period depends on how long the tenant has lived in the property: three months if the tenant has lived there for less than five years, six months for five to eight years, and nine months for more than eight years. The notice must be in writing (Schriftform), clearly state the qualifying person and their specific housing need, and be delivered personally or by registered post.

If the landlord asserts Eigenbedarf but then does not move in (or does not move in the stated person) within a reasonable period after the tenant leaves, the tenant has grounds for damages — including compensation for moving costs, higher rent elsewhere, and potentially the return of the apartment if it remains empty. Misuse of Eigenbedarf is taken seriously by German courts.

Tenants faced with an Eigenbedarfskündigung should seek advice from a Mieterverein (tenants' association) or a specialist Mietrechtanwalt (tenant lawyer). The justification can often be challenged on factual or procedural grounds. Even valid notices can sometimes be opposed on grounds of special hardship (besondere Härtegründe) — for example, a long-term tenant of advanced age with no realistic alternative housing.

Key Facts

Legal basis§573 BGB — qualifying persons strictly defined
Notice period3 months (<5 years), 6 months (5–8 years), 9 months (>8 years)
Notice formWritten, naming specific person and specific need
Hardship defenceTenant can oppose on grounds of special hardship (§574 BGB)
Abuse consequencesDamages for moving costs, higher rent, possible return of property
⚠ Common Mistake: Leaving the property without challenging a notice you believe is false. If you have evidence the landlord is not actually moving in, consult a tenant lawyer immediately. False Eigenbedarf notices are a criminal misuse and the landlord can be liable for all your losses.
💡 Expert Tip: If you receive an Eigenbedarfskündigung, immediately write to the landlord confirming receipt and asking for documentary proof of the stated family member's need. This creates a paper trail and may cause a landlord with weak grounds to withdraw.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the landlord claim Eigenbedarf for a newly purchased property?

Yes, but this is limited. The Kündigungssperrfrist (eviction moratorium) prevents a new owner from exercising Eigenbedarf for three years after acquiring a property that was a rental at the time of purchase (and up to 10 years in some high-demand cities by local regulation). This protects sitting tenants from immediate eviction by new owners.

What is a Mieterverein and how can it help?

A Mieterverein is a tenant protection association. For an annual membership fee of approximately €80–120, members receive free legal advice, document review, and representation in tenancy disputes from qualified Mietrechtanwälte. The investment is worthwhile for any long-term renter in Germany.

Can I ask the landlord to pay my moving costs?

German law does not require a landlord to pay moving costs for a valid Eigenbedarfskündigung, but courts increasingly award some compensation in cases where the tenant's hardship is disproportionate. Negotiating a financial settlement — perhaps three to six months' rent — to vacate without a legal battle is also common practice.
