Kaufvertrag (Purchase Contract)

The German notarially certified purchase contract — the legally binding deed of sale for real estate, which must be executed before a notary (Notar) to be valid.

Home › Legal Glossary › Kaufvertrag (Purchase Contract)
TL;DR The German notarially certified purchase contract — the legally binding deed of sale for real estate, which must be executed before a notary (Notar) to be valid.

The Kaufvertrag (purchase contract) for German real estate must be notarially certified (notariell beurkundet) by a licensed German notary (Notar). Unlike in many countries, a private written contract is not sufficient to transfer ownership of real estate in Germany — notarial certification is a mandatory formal requirement under §311b BGB (German Civil Code). Without it, the contract is void.

German notarial certification means the notary reads the entire contract aloud to all parties, answers questions, and only then signs and seals the document. The notary is a neutral officer of the legal system — they represent neither buyer nor seller but ensure the contract is lawful. The notary also coordinates land registry entries, tax clearance, and payment of the purchase price.

A typical German Kaufvertrag includes: full description of the property with land register reference (Grundbuchauszug), the agreed purchase price, payment terms, the Auflassungsvormerkung (priority notice protecting the buyer), handover date and conditions, representations on encumbrances, and the Auflassung (formal agreement to transfer title). All must be read aloud at the notarial appointment.

German law implies a duty to disclose material defects known to the seller. Sellers frequently try to limit liability with "gekauft wie gesehen" (sold as seen) clauses. While these are valid for visible defects, they cannot exclude liability for deliberately concealed defects (arglistig verschwiegene Mängel). Buyers should commission an independent building survey before signing.

After signing, the notary secures the Auflassungsvormerkung in the Grundbuch, requests tax clearance from the Finanzamt, and only then arranges the final Auflassung (title transfer) once the purchase price is paid. The full registration process typically takes eight to twelve weeks. The buyer is effectively protected from the moment the Auflassungsvormerkung is registered.

⚠ Common Mistake: Signing a private purchase agreement (Vorvertrag) before the notarial appointment and treating it as legally binding. Pre-contractual agreements for real estate are not enforceable in Germany — only the notarially certified Kaufvertrag has legal force.
💡 Expert Tip: Ask the notary to send you the draft Kaufvertrag at least two weeks before the appointment. You have a right to review it in advance, and a German property lawyer can identify any unfavourable clauses. Do not accept a draft the night before signing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the Kaufvertrag be in English?

No. Notarial deeds in Germany must be in German. A certified translation may accompany the German deed for a foreign buyer's reference, but only the German text is legally operative. If you do not speak German, the notary must arrange for a certified interpreter to attend.

What is the Auflassung?

The Auflassung is the formal agreement to transfer ownership of the property, contained within the Kaufvertrag. It is the declaration by seller and buyer to the notary that title should pass. The Auflassung is registered in the Grundbuch as the final step of the transfer, after the purchase price is paid and tax clearance is obtained.

What happens if the seller discovers a defect after signing?

If the defect was known to the seller before signing and was not disclosed, the buyer can rescind the contract and claim damages regardless of any "sold as seen" clause. If it is a genuinely new discovery by both parties, the contractual allocation of risk applies. In both cases, seek specialist German property law advice promptly.
