Mietvertrag (Rental Agreement)

A German rental contract — the agreement governing residential or commercial tenancy, with significant tenant protections under the BGB and Mietrechtsreform.

Home › Legal Glossary › Mietvertrag (Rental Agreement)
TL;DR A German rental contract — the agreement governing residential or commercial tenancy, with significant tenant protections under the BGB and Mietrechtsreform.

The Mietvertrag (rental contract) governs the relationship between landlord (Vermieter) and tenant (Mieter) in Germany. German tenancy law is heavily weighted in favour of tenants and forms one of the most protective rental frameworks in Europe. Key protections are embedded in the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB — German Civil Code) and cannot be contracted away to the tenant's detriment.

German residential Mietverträge are typically indefinite in duration — unlike many countries, fixed-term tenancies are only valid if the landlord can demonstrate a specific reason (e.g. the landlord will need the property for personal use). Indefinite tenancies give tenants long-term security and limit the grounds on which landlords can terminate.

A German landlord can terminate a residential tenancy (with proper notice) only on limited grounds: own use (Eigenbedarf — for themselves or close family), persistent significant breach by the tenant, or if the landlord intends to undertake works making the property temporarily uninhabitable. Termination simply to re-let at a higher rent is not permitted. Notice periods range from three to nine months depending on how long the tenant has lived there.

The Mietspiegel (rent index) is a locally published table of average rents for comparable properties, produced by municipalities. In many German cities, landlords cannot charge rent more than 10–20% above the Mietspiegel average without specific justification. In areas designated as Mietpreisbremse (rent brake) zones, new tenancy rents are capped at 10% above the local Mietspiegel.

Security deposits (Kaution) for residential tenancies are capped at three months' cold rent (Kaltmiete — excluding heating costs). The landlord must hold the Kaution in a separate interest-bearing account (not mixed with their own funds) and return it within a reasonable period after the tenancy ends — typically three to six months — deducting only genuine damage beyond normal wear and tear.

Key Facts

DurationTypically indefinite — fixed-term valid only for specific reasons
Termination groundsEigenbedarf, persistent breach, major works — not rent increase
Notice period3–9 months depending on length of tenancy
Rent capMietpreisbremse zones: max 10% above Mietspiegel
Deposit capMaximum 3 months' Kaltmiete, held in separate account
⚠ Common Mistake: Paying a deposit of more than three months' Kaltmiete. German law caps this strictly. Landlords demanding four or five months' deposit are acting unlawfully and tenants can recover the excess.
💡 Expert Tip: Before signing, check the local Mietspiegel for the property's area and type. If the rent significantly exceeds the index, you may have grounds to challenge the rent during the tenancy (Mieterhöhung contestation) and potentially recover overpaid amounts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sublet my German apartment?

You may sublet part of the apartment with the landlord's consent. The landlord can only refuse consent if they have an important reason (e.g. the subtenant would significantly overcrowd the apartment). Subletting the entire apartment without consent is a breach of contract and can justify termination of your tenancy.

What is the Nebenkostenabrechnung?

The Nebenkostenabrechnung is the annual utility cost reconciliation statement — showing what you actually owe for heating, hot water, building insurance, caretaking, and other shared services, compared to your monthly advance payments. Landlords must issue this within 12 months of the end of the accounting year. If they miss this deadline, they cannot charge additional costs beyond your advance payments.

What are my rights at the end of the tenancy regarding decorating?

Schönheitsreparaturen (cosmetic repairs) clauses in many German tenancy contracts require tenants to repaint on leaving. The legality of such clauses depends on their precise wording — overly broad or rigid formulations have repeatedly been struck down by German courts. If in doubt, consult a Mieterverein (tenants' association) before carrying out expensive repainting.
