WEG (Wohnungseigentumsgesetz — Condominium Law)

Germany's Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG) governs the co-ownership of apartment buildings, setting out the rights and obligations of individual unit owners and the Eigentümergemeinschaft (owners' community).

TL;DR Germany's Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG) governs the co-ownership of apartment buildings, setting out the rights and obligations of individual unit owners and the Eigentümergemeinschaft (owners' community).

The Wohnungseigentumsgesetz (WEG — Condominium Ownership Act) is the German law governing apartment ownership in multi-unit buildings. When you buy a flat (Eigentumswohnung) in Germany, you become both the owner of your individual unit (Sondereigentum) and a co-owner of the building's common parts (Gemeinschaftseigentum). The WEG defines the rights and obligations of each owner and the collective body (Wohnungseigentümergemeinschaft — WEG).

The WEG community makes collective decisions at the annual owners' meeting (Eigentümerversammlung) about building management, major repairs, and budget allocation. Each owner votes in proportion to their co-ownership share (Miteigentumsanteil — MEA). The WEG community is managed day-to-day by a professional Verwalter (property manager) appointed by the owners.

The monthly Hausgeld (service charge) paid by each owner covers: building insurance, cleaning and caretaking, maintenance and repairs, heating of common areas, the Instandhaltungsrücklage (maintenance reserve fund), and the Verwalter's fee. The Hausgeld is separate from the heating costs for the individual apartment, which are typically billed separately based on actual consumption.

A major WEG reform (WEG-Gesetz reform 2020) significantly modernised the law. Key changes include: making it easier to pass resolutions (simple majority for many matters previously requiring unanimity), allowing owners to make structural modifications to their unit for disability access and modern communications, and strengthening protections against abusive Verwalter conduct.

When buying a German flat, always review the Teilungserklärung (declaration of division — the founding document of the WEG), the current Wirtschaftsplan (budget), the Abrechnungen (accounts for the last three years), the meeting minutes (Protokolle), and the Instandhaltungsrücklage balance. A healthy reserve fund and a professionally managed WEG are key quality indicators.

⚠ Common Mistake: Buying a WEG apartment without requesting the Instandhaltungsrücklage balance. A depleted reserve fund means an imminent Sonderumlage (special levy) for major works — potentially thousands of euros per unit.
💡 Expert Tip: Read the last three years of Eigentümerversammlungs-Protokolle (meeting minutes) before buying. They reveal pending disputes, planned major works, management problems, and neighbour conflicts that no estate agent will volunteer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the WEG decide to restrict short-term rentals of my apartment?

Yes. Under the reformed WEG, owners can pass a resolution restricting or banning the letting of apartments for short-term tourist use (Kurzzeitvermietung). A three-quarters majority of all co-ownership shares is required. Always check whether such a resolution exists before buying a flat you intend to let on Airbnb or similar platforms.

What is the Teilungserklärung?

The Teilungserklärung is the founding document of the WEG — it defines the boundaries of each Sondereigentum unit, the allocation of the Gemeinschaftseigentum, each owner's Miteigentumsanteil, and any special use rights (Sondernutzungsrechte — e.g. a parking space or garden section allocated to a specific flat). It is registered in the Grundbuch and cannot be changed without unanimous consent.

What happens if the WEG is badly managed?

Owners can remove a Verwalter by a simple majority vote and appoint a new one. If the Verwalter is acting fraudulently or in breach of their duties, individual owners can apply to the courts. Since the 2020 reform, the powers to challenge and remove underperforming managers have been strengthened.
