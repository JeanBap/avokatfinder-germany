Aufenthaltserlaubnis (Residence Permit)

The German temporary residence permit for non-EU nationals — required for stays exceeding 90 days, with various categories for employment, study, family, and business.

Home › Legal Glossary › Aufenthaltserlaubnis (Residence Permit)
TL;DR The German temporary residence permit for non-EU nationals — required for stays exceeding 90 days, with various categories for employment, study, family, and business.

The Aufenthaltserlaubnis (residence permit) is a temporary permit allowing non-EU/EEA nationals to live in Germany beyond 90 days. It is issued for specific purposes — employment, self-employment, study, family reunification, or humanitarian reasons — and must be applied for at the local Ausländerbehörde (foreigners' authority) before the 90-day visa-free (or visa) period expires.

EU and EEA citizens do not need a formal Aufenthaltserlaubnis but must register their residence (Anmeldung) and notify the Ausländerbehörde for stays of more than three months. They are entitled to residence by virtue of the EU Freedom of Movement Directive. Swiss nationals have comparable rights under the bilateral Switzerland-EU agreement.

The most relevant permit categories for expat property buyers and professionals include: §18 AufenthG (employment with a German employer), §21 AufenthG (self-employment and freelance), §7 Abs.1 S.3 AufenthG (general residence for those with sufficient means), and the §18c AufenthG (Job Seeker Visa). The permitted activities vary significantly between categories — always check what work you are permitted to do.

Required documents vary by permit category but typically include: valid passport, biometric photograph, proof of accommodation (Meldebescheinigung), proof of financial means (bank statements or employment contract), health insurance documentation, proof of German language skills (usually B1 level or higher for most permits), and any employment-specific qualifications.

The Niederlassungserlaubnis (permanent settlement permit) is a more secure status available after typically five years of continuous legal residence. It provides greater employment flexibility, long-term security, and is a stepping stone to naturalisation as a German citizen. Meeting the conditions — sufficient income, pension contributions, adequate German language skills, and no criminal record — is important throughout the residency period.

⚠ Common Mistake: Starting work before your Aufenthaltserlaubnis with a work permission component is issued. Working without authorisation is a serious immigration violation that can result in deportation and a re-entry ban.
💡 Expert Tip: Apply for the Aufenthaltserlaubnis at least six to eight weeks before your current permission expires. Ausländerbehörde appointment systems in major cities (Berlin, Munich, Hamburg) are heavily booked. Many offer online bookings — book as early as possible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I buy property in Germany on a tourist visa?

Yes — there are no nationality restrictions on buying German real estate. However, owning property does not itself confer a right to live in Germany. If you want to move to Germany, you need a separate residence permit. Property ownership is not a qualifying basis for residence under German immigration law.

What is the Blaue Karte EU?

The EU Blue Card (Blaue Karte EU) is a special residence permit for highly qualified non-EU professionals with a university degree and a qualifying employment contract (above a minimum salary threshold). It offers faster routes to permanent residence (21 months with B1 German, 33 months with A1) and enhanced family reunification rights.

What happens if my Aufenthaltserlaubnis expires before I can renew it?

Under §81 AufenthG, if you apply for renewal before expiry, the permit is deemed extended (Fiktionsbescheinigung) while the application is pending. This legal fiction protects you from illegal residence status during the renewal process, provided you applied in time. Always apply before expiry — not after.
